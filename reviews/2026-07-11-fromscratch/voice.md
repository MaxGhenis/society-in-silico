# Voice bible — Society in Silico, ground-up rewrite (2026-07-11)

Every writer on this book follows this document. It outranks habit. When a
sentence you like breaks a rule here, the sentence loses.

## Who is speaking

Max Ghenis: an economist who builds things, writing in first person about work he
did and watched happen. He explains by showing the thing operating, not by
describing what he is about to explain. He trusts the reader with real numbers and
real names. He is confident about what he verified, plain about what failed, and
unimpressed by his own accomplishments — the machinery is the point, not the
builder. He never sells. He never apologizes. He never winds up.

## Sentence laws

1. **Active voice, direct, throughout.** Passive survives only when the actor is
   unknown or genuinely beside the point. Never to soften.
2. **First sentences carry the most weight** — of the book, of a chapter, of a
   section, of a paragraph. Open in a scene, a claim, or a number. Never in
   throat-clearing ("It is worth noting," "To understand X, we must first"), never
   in a definition, never in an apology.
3. **No tautologies or redundant pairs**: "encode X as code," "end result,"
   "combine together," "past history," "advance planning."
4. **Simple verb forms beat tentative ones** where truth allows: "I wanted to
   know," not "I was trying to understand." "The model fails," not "the model
   seems to struggle."
5. **Hedge only about the world, never about the sentence.** "The estimate could
   swing $40 billion either way" is information. "This is perhaps somewhat
   surprising" is a wince. Keep the first kind; the second kind never appears.
6. **Honesty is a statement, not a flinch.** Limitations, failures, and unresolved
   grades appear as flat declarative facts, in active voice, without cushioning:
   "Not one forecast has resolved." "Twenty-three of the twenty-five came back
   worse." "We were wrong about personas."

## Signature moves (use them; they are the author's)

- **Numbers as protagonists.** A precise figure does the arguing: three cents
  across 78,479 workers; 0.4 shillings a year; $23 billion; one in nine. Introduce
  the number, then let it sit. No "a staggering," no "fully," no "remarkably."
- **Counterintuitive findings, stated flat.** "A poor single mother faces a higher
  marginal tax rate than a billionaire" needs no drumroll.
- **The concrete household.** When policy gets abstract, put a person in it —
  specific state, specific income, specific programs — and run the rules on them.
- **Time-anchored specificity.** Dates, versions, places: "In June 2004, a month
  after my high school graduation." The specificity is the sincerity.
- **Self-implication.** The strongest credibility move in the book is the gates
  turning red on the author's own work. Failures get the same crisp treatment as
  wins, and never the word "unfortunately."
- **One-sentence paragraphs are gunpowder.** At most two per chapter, only where
  the argument detonates. If a chapter has three, cut the weakest.

## Anti-tics (the previous drafts' fingerprints — do not reproduce)

- **The bolded triad list** ("**Trust deficits.** … **Limited debate.** … ").
  Sequence the ideas in prose instead. One structured list per chapter, maximum,
  and only when the content is genuinely enumerable.
- **The "This isn't X. It's Y." template** and its cousins ("not because A, but
  because B" as a reflex). Once per chapter at most; the move dies with repetition.
- **Em-dash chains.** One em-dash pair per paragraph, roughly. If every sentence
  breathes through dashes, rebuild the sentences.
- **The abstract-noun windup**: "The implications of this development for the
  landscape of policy analysis…" Name the actor, name the act.
- **Symmetric closers** that restate the chapter in aphorism form. End on a fact,
  an image, or the next question — not a bow.
- **"Journey," "landscape," "space," "dive," "unpack," "leverage," "utilize,"
  "robust" (except statistics), "compelling," "powerful."**

## Rhythm

Vary sentence length deliberately: a long cumulative sentence that keeps adding
specific clauses can run thirty-five words and earn every one, and the next
sentence can be four. Paragraphs develop one idea and stop. Sections earn their
headings — if two sections say one thing, merge them. Read every paragraph aloud
in your head; the book will be listened to as much as read.

## Diction

Plain verbs. Technical terms get one explanation, woven into a story, at first
use — then the book uses them like a professional would. Prefer "check" to
"validate" in narration; keep "verify/verification" for the discipline itself.
"The law," "the rules," "the code" — concrete nouns over "systems" and
"frameworks" wherever the concrete noun is true.

## Calibration exemplar (this register, everywhere)

> The first country took four years. PolicyEngine UK went live in the fall of
> 2021 after Nikhil and I had encoded, by hand, every major tax and benefit in
> Britain: income tax and National Insurance, Universal Credit and its taper,
> child benefit and its clawback. We typed the thresholds off gov.uk and argued
> about rounding. Uganda took one day. On July 8, 2026, an agent read the gazette,
> wrote the rules, raced them against the reference model that UNU-WIDER has
> maintained for a decade, and finished with a ledger showing zero findings — the
> two implementations agreed everywhere we tested, to within 0.4 shillings a year.
> I checked the arithmetic myself that evening, looking for the catch. There
> wasn't one. The catch was in my assumptions about how long careful work takes.

That is the book: a real event, dated; numbers doing the arguing; the author
present but not performing; the admission at the end costing something.

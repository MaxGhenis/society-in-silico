## Facts

### The €643 cold open
- The first race of a Belgium social-security-contributions encoding against EUROMOD (the EU's reference tax-benefit model) disagreed: the encoding computed roughly €20.9 billion in employee and employer contributions across a simulated population of workers; EUROMOD came out €643 higher.
- The €643 alone cannot say whether that is success or failure; it is consistent with several underlying realities: floating-point dust (hundreds of thousands of sub-cent rounding differences summed); a single catastrophic error (one worker off by €643 while all others match to the euro); a small systematic bias (a rule applied a hair too generously to everyone); or the worst case — large errors in opposite directions canceling almost perfectly on the way to a reassuring total (looks best from a distance).
- Earlier-chapter callback (CBO / ACA): when CBO scored the Affordable Care Act in 2010, its projection of the total uninsured rate landed within a percentage point of the outcome while getting the composition badly wrong — overstating exchange enrollment by more than half and understating Medicaid by nearly as much, the two errors partly canceling; the total was approximately right for the wrong reasons. If the only test is whether two totals are close, you cannot distinguish a right model from a flatteringly-wrong one.
- Resolution: they compared workers, not totals. For each of the 78,479 simulated workers they lined up the encoding's contribution against EUROMOD's and took the difference; the largest single-worker discrepancy in the entire population was three cents a year (not on average — three cents at the very worst). Every worker matched to within a rounding error, and the €643 aggregate was exactly what it looked like once examined per-unit.

### The ladder of checks
- In the Axiom Foundation pipeline [@axiomfoundation2026], the first gate is the money-atom check, and it is merge-blocking. Every monetary obligation (each rate, threshold, taper, and cap that actually moves money) must be grounded in a quoted excerpt from the governing source — statute, regulation, agency manual — quoted verbatim with a pointer to where it lives. An amount with no provision behind it fails the build the way a syntax error does.
- The finished Belgian encoding carried 539 distinct monetary obligations; the gate demanded a source excerpt for every one; the count of ungrounded obligations was zero. The interesting part is not that it reached zero but that the gate is configured so the count can only ever fall.
- Grounding is necessary but not sufficient: a number can be faithfully quoted and still be wired into the wrong formula, or the right formula with the wrong sign. So the encoding must also compile, pass the companion tests that ship beside every rule, and clear a proof pass checking the logic holds together (pieces refer to quantities that exist, in units that match, along paths that resolve). These gates establish internal coherence and external sourcing, not correctness.
- For correctness the encoding is raced against an oracle: an independent implementation of the same law (usually built by other people, often over many years) whose answers are treated as a second opinion the team did not write and cannot quietly influence.
- Oracles in use: TAXSIM, the NBER calculator that has anchored academic tax research for four decades [@feenberg1993taxsim]; the classic PolicyEngine engine; UKMOD, for the United Kingdom [@ukmod2020]; EUROMOD, for EU member states [@sutherland2013euromod]; the SOUTHMOD family UNU-WIDER maintains for a growing list of developing economies [@unuwider2026southmod]; and, wherever a tax authority publishes one, its own official calculator.
- Chapter 3 laid out three validation levels — component, aggregate, predictive. The oracle program industrializes the first, component validation: from spot-checking one case against an IRS worksheet to replaying entire populations through two engines and demanding agreement on every record — component validation grown teeth.
- Why race whole populations rather than a curated handful: a hand-written suite tests only the situations its author imagined (the married couple, the pensioner, the three-child family) and says nothing about the unimagined case (the worker crossing two thresholds in one year, the household in the seam between two rules); replaying a full calibrated population through two independent engines tests those too, because the population contains them whether or not anyone anticipated them.

### What parity looks like (UK)
- The UK is where the encodings and the oracle agree most exactly. For a deterministic calculation there is a single right answer, and a tolerance band is mostly a comfortable place for a wrong answer to hide.
- UK income tax matches to the pound (not within a tolerance) across five test suites, including the awkward withdrawal of the personal allowance — the taper that claws back the tax-free band from high earners and produces the marginal-rate spike that trips up informal tax advice.
- The Scottish income tax (its own six-band structure) matches on nine cases out of nine.
- Savings income and dividend income, each taxed on its own schedule with its own allowances, match exactly.
- National Insurance matches or is explained: where the encoding and UKMOD diverge, the divergence traces to a documented convention — UKMOD computes contributions weekly then annualizes, while the encoding works in annual terms — and once you account for the convention, the two reconcile.
- Universal Credit: of nineteen test cases, eight matched. The eleven gaps are not errors: UKMOD applies a stochastic take-up draw (deciding at random, household by household, whether an eligible family actually claims, because in reality many eligible families never do), while the encoding computes statutory entitlement (what the household is owed if it claims). Those are different quantities by construction, and every one of the eleven mismatches resolves precisely to that difference — the two systems answer two different questions.

### Disposition (the load-bearing word)
- In the conformance program a raw mismatch is neither a failure nor a pass but an open question that stays open until closed with evidence.
- An explanation is a checked artifact, not a shrug: an arithmetic reconciliation that reproduces the gap to the penny, a citation to the statutory provision the oracle implements differently, or a documented account of an oracle's own modeling convention (like the Universal Credit take-up draw). A mismatch is either dispositioned or unexplained, and only unexplained mismatches count against the encoding.
- Standing UK result (the number that matters, not the raw match rate): across every compared surface, 100% of mismatches explained, zero unexplained.

### What parity looks like (Belgium)
- The regional and municipal surcharges on top of Belgian income tax match nine cases out of nine.
- A greenfield encoding of student study allowances (a body of rules with no prior implementation anywhere to copy from) matches six out of six.
- The contributions match per worker to three cents.
- Every mismatch on the Belgian conformance board carries its disposition, and none is attributed to an error on the encoding's side. "Clean" here means everything that disagreed was run to ground and written down, not that nothing disagreed.

### When the oracle is wrong
- The dispositions discipline has an unplanned consequence: if every mismatch must be chased to evidence, sometimes the evidence exonerates the encoding and implicates the reference model. Racing a fresh, exhaustively grounded implementation against a hand-maintained model, now and then the fresh implementation is right where the mature one is wrong.
- What makes that possible is exhaustiveness, not cleverness: nobody maintaining a mature model by hand replays tens of thousands of records against a second independent engine on every change (until recently the compute and tooling were too costly); when you do, an inconsistency that surfaces in a handful of unusual cases stops hiding in the tail and shows up as a specific record with a specific wrong number a person can investigate.
- Chasing one mismatch through the UK savings rules, the discrepancy was not in the encoding: UKMOD was mishandling an interaction involving the personal savings allowance (a corner where two provisions meet and combined behavior is easy to get subtly wrong). They wrote it up as the discipline demands (inputs, the statutory result expected and why, the reference model's actual output, the provision each implemented) and filed it publicly as an issue on the UKMOD-PUBLIC repository for its maintainers to weigh [@ukmod2026bugfiling].
- A second, stranger case: running the EUROMOD comparison, identical input cases sometimes scored differently depending on where they sat in a batch (the same household, the same year, two answers determined by neighbors in the queue rather than the case's own circumstances) — the fingerprint of state leaking between records meant to be independent; root-caused to a batch-processing contamination bug in the adapter layer that runs cases through the model; reported to the maintainers with a reproduction in hand.
- These are not gotcha stories: the reference models are why the encodings can be trusted at all; strip the independent second opinion away and "the machine says so" is exactly the epistemic position the project exists to escape. TAXSIM has been maintained for forty years; EUROMOD and its national variants represent decades of institutional work. That a model of that size contains a bug is not a scandal (every large model does, the encodings emphatically included), and finding one is not a victory over its authors — it is collaboration running in both directions: the reference model gets free, specific, evidence-backed QA from a system precise enough to notice a three-cent gap and disciplined enough to chase it to cause. Openness means errors flow both ways; bugs filed upstream and downstream, both models better.

### A one-way valve on correctness (the ratchet)
- The hardest honest objection to a public rules layer is not that it will be wrong on launch day but that it will rot: laws change yearly; the failure mode of shared infrastructure is quiet staleness — encodings drifting out of date, coverage eroding at the edges, a model accurate on ship and subtly wrong three budgets later with no one the wiser. A verification suite that runs once and is admired is no defense; a suite wired into a ratchet is.
- The conformance gates are configured as one-way valves: coverage (the number of statutory surfaces under test against an oracle) may only rise — a change that would drop a jurisdiction or program below its current tested coverage fails CI and cannot merge. The count of unexplained mismatches may only fall — a change introducing a new unexplained divergence, or quietly un-explaining a previously dispositioned one, fails too. The count of ungrounded monetary obligations may only fall toward the zero it already reached.
- The valves hold because the full conformance suite runs on every change, not on a schedule someone must remember. When a new budget moves a threshold, the new value lands as a dated addition beside the old one and must pass against the oracle for its own year before merging (last year's cases checked against last year's law, this year's against this year's); nothing is overwritten in place, so nothing quietly falls out of coverage. The moment the encodings drift from the law is the moment the build stops going green.
- This converts an anxious, permanently-behind human maintenance problem into a mechanical invariant. Staleness does not become impossible (a rule can still go un-encoded, a new program can still escape coverage), but silent staleness does, and silence was always the dangerous part. The ratchet makes no promise the encodings are complete; it promises the narrower, more enforceable thing — correctness, once won and checked, cannot be lost again without the build going red. Weaker than "always current," much stronger than "trust us to keep it current."

### The gates are for us first
- It would be comfortable to describe all of this as a wall against untrustworthy AI; that is true but not the honest emphasis. The gates catch the builders' own mistakes first, and the most useful report is the times they turned red on their authors.
- B1 probe (recounted here): they took twenty-five existing modules, regenerated each fresh from its source, and compared the new output against the in-production version; regeneration was almost free, about eight cents of compute per module; of the twenty-five, twenty-three came back with the old, in-production version correct and the regenerated version worse — most often introducing naming instability (renamed variables that would silently break every downstream consumer). The premise (encodings are disposable cache) failed. Encodings are not cache; they are durable artifacts with provenance, and regeneration, as currently run, is not yet trustworthy enough to be automatic. Known not by advance reasoning but because a gate compared regenerated output to a known-good baseline and 23 of 25 came back wrong.
- Deeper lesson: an encoding is an answer with a history (which source, which version of the law, which call was made where the text was genuinely ambiguous); regenerating from scratch discards that history and produces a new artifact that looks every bit as authoritative while quietly disagreeing with the one every downstream system relies on. Durability is the property that lets a consumer trust yesterday's number still means the same today, and that when it changes it changed because the law did.
- Same lesson at the Chapter 8 benchmark: an early layer that narrated each result in prose was caught inventing derivations — in one case describing elderly enrollees as beneficiaries of a "non-elderly expansion" — and any audit reading those confident narratives inherited the fabrication whole [@policybench2026]; the fix was to ground the explanation in the engine's actual internals and add a validator that rejects any mechanism the underlying trace does not support. Even the audit layer needs ground truth; you give the judges traces, not prose.
- And when a US-calibrated population was adapted to the United Kingdom and written up, a summary statistic that no run had actually produced (a fabricated number) found its way into a draft and was caught at a verification gate before publication, and removed.
- None of these is an embarrassing exception to a spotless record — they are the record; a verification regime that only ever vindicates its builders is a marketing department with test coverage; the gates earn their standing by how often they turn red on their own authors.

### The admission rule and the closing hook
- Underneath all of it sits a single rule (reproduced verbatim in Arguments): simulation is admissible only where its verification chain terminates in ground truth — a forecast score, an oracle parity, a statutory exactness, or a calibrated marginal.
- Each terminus is a different kind of true thing checked a different way: an oracle parity (an independent implementation agreeing on every record — this chapter's subject); a statutory exactness (the money-atom carried to its end, an obligation traced to the quoted provision that fixes it); a calibrated marginal (an input distribution reconciled against a number the government actually counted, so the population is pinned to something real); and a forecast score (a prediction with a published interval, graded only later when the official figure lands).
- They are not interchangeable but share the one property that makes any admissible: each bottoms out somewhere the model's own confidence gets no vote. Where the chain exists and holds, the estimate is admissible; where it does not, the estimate — however fluent, cheap, or fast — is confident fiction, and the honest move is to mark the boundary.
- Closing hook: everything here rests on the existence of an oracle (a UKMOD to disagree with, a EUROMOD to file bugs against, an official calculator to match to the pound). The chain terminates cleanly for the UK, Belgium, and the US because a second opinion already exists; but most of the world's tax and benefit law has never been encoded, and for much of it no reference model exists at all — no calculator to race, no mature implementation to disagree with, no independent answer to check. What becomes of the discipline where there is nothing? (→ next chapter.)

## Arguments

1. A single aggregate number (€643) is consistent with a dozen underlying realities — float dust, one catastrophic error, a small systematic bias, or large offsetting errors canceling — and the last looks best from a distance while being the worst.
2. Aggregate agreement can hide anything; per-unit comparison hides nothing. Per-unit verification is the only test that distinguishes a right model from a flatteringly-wrong one (the CBO/ACA aggregate-right-wrong-composition case is the cautionary precedent).
3. Trust is the wrong frame: you verify, one unit at a time, against something true independently of the machine, and wire the verification so tightly that a failing encoding cannot ship. Verification is not a QA step bolted onto the method — it is the method.
4. An encoding no one has checked per-determination against ground truth is not a cheaper validated model; it is confident fiction with good production values.
5. The ladder of checks: money-atom grounding (merge-blocking, count can only fall) is necessary but not sufficient; the encoding must also compile, pass companion tests, and clear a proof pass — establishing internal coherence and external sourcing, but not correctness.
6. Correctness is established only by racing against an oracle (an independent implementation, a second opinion the team did not write and cannot influence); the oracle program industrializes component validation — replaying whole populations through two engines and demanding agreement on every record.
7. Race whole populations, not curated cases: a hand-written suite tests only imagined situations; a calibrated population contains the unimagined pathological case that would otherwise ship undetected.
8. "Exactly" means exactly: for a deterministic calculation there is a single right answer, and a tolerance band is mostly a comfortable place for a wrong answer to hide.
9. A raw mismatch is an open question (a "disposition"), closed only by a checked artifact (an arithmetic reconciliation to the penny, a statutory citation, or a documented oracle convention); only unexplained mismatches count against the encoding, so the result that matters is "100% explained, zero unexplained," not the raw match rate.
10. The dispositions discipline sometimes exonerates the encoding and implicates the reference model; exhaustiveness (not cleverness) surfaces a mature model's bug as a specific record with a specific wrong number.
11. Finding a bug in a decades-maintained reference model is not a scandal or a victory but collaboration in both directions; openness means errors flow both ways.
12. The ratchet is a one-way valve — coverage only rises, unexplained mismatches only fall, ungrounded obligations only fall — enforced by running the full suite on every change with dated additions beside old values; it converts staleness from a human maintenance problem into a mechanical invariant, making silent staleness impossible. It is weaker than "always current" and much stronger than "trust us to keep it current."
13. The gates catch the builders' own mistakes first; a verification regime that only vindicates its authors is a marketing department with test coverage, and the gates earn standing by how often they turn red on their authors.
14. The admission rule (verbatim): "Simulation is admissible only where its verification chain terminates in ground truth—a forecast score, an oracle parity, a statutory exactness, a calibrated marginal." Each terminus is a different true thing (oracle parity, statutory exactness, calibrated marginal, forecast score), and each bottoms out somewhere the model's own confidence gets no vote.
15. Everything rests on the existence of an oracle; where no reference model exists — most of the world's tax and benefit law — the verification chain does not terminate, which is the boundary question the next chapter takes up.

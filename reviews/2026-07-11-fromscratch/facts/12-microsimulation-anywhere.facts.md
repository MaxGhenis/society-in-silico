## Facts

### The SOUTHMOD week (dated)
- In the second week of July 2026, five countries acquired public, inspectable models of their own tax and benefit systems; none had one before; the work took about a week.
- Historically a national tax-benefit model has been the work of years — a team, a budget, a data-sharing agreement, a maintenance commitment measured in decades — which is why such models lived inside finance ministries and a few well-funded institutes and why most of the world has none.
- Two things had to be true at once for the week to matter: that a model could be built this fast, and that each one arrived carrying the evidence for whether it was right. Most of the chapter is about the second condition, because without it the first is a liability.
- It started July 8, with two countries finished the same day.
- Ghana first (GHAMOD, the Ghanaian member of SOUTHMOD — UNU-WIDER's family of tax-benefit models covering fourteen developing economies [@unuwider2026southmod]): its 2025 tax-and-benefit system, encoded provision by provision and tested across its full surface against GHAMOD; the comparison produced thirteen findings — thirteen catalogued places where the encoded statute and the reference model disagreed, each run down, explained, and posted to a public ledger anyone can open and read [@rulespecgh2026findings].
- Uganda finished the same day, in a single day of work, reusing the recipe Ghana had just proven; its ledger recorded no findings at all. The encoding was, in the phrase recorded in that ledger, statutorily exact everywhere tested; the widest disagreement anywhere in the suite was four-tenths of one Ugandan shilling per year (rounding, nothing else).
- Thirteen and zero, produced the same day by the same method; neither is an obvious headline, and both are good news — but only once you know what a finding is.
- By the end of the week there were five: Zambia landed on the 9th; Ethiopia landed overnight; Rwanda closed it out. Two more sat at the edge of the week and matter more than the tidy five.
- Zambia (encoded in about a day, checked against MicroZAMOD): eight findings; the first of the five to turn up divergences on both the tax and benefit sides rather than the tax side alone — among them a definition of the pay-as-you-earn base and a set of excise rates carried a year stale. Eight findings on a first pass across an entire system is low, and straddling both sides is informative: the seam where the two halves meet is where encodings and reference models most often drift apart, and Zambia was the first case with enough surface tested to see the drift on both.
- Ethiopia (a single overnight run, compared against ETMOD): two findings, both in the same place — the new minimum alternative tax introduced only months earlier by Proclamation 1395/2025. A statute that new has barely been implemented by anyone, and two first-pass disagreements about how it works are close to what you would predict — both of the understandable kind (reasonable readings of a fresh law that differ, rather than arithmetic simply wrong).
- Rwanda (closed the week, against RWAMOD): five findings; the first lane where the comparison was executed live rather than replayed from a fixture — the encoded model and the reference model were handed the same eighty-six cases and asked to agree; the run surfaced the five findings, and once those were dispositioned the two agreed across all eighty-six.
- Tanzania is under way.
- Nigeria has begun — and Nigeria has no oracle: it is not one of the SOUTHMOD countries, and no other reference model or administrative ground truth is available to check the encoding against. The book's rule is that a simulation may be believed only where its chain of verification ends in something real — a reference model it matches, a statute it reproduces exactly, or a number the world will later publish. Nigeria's chain does not yet terminate in any of those; the encoding can still be built, and it has been, but the claim that it is correct stays weaker than the same claim for Ghana or Uganda, and the honest thing is to say so in the same breath that reports the encoding exists. The boundary of the method is part of the method.

### What a finding is
- A finding is not a bug report or a failure notice; it is a dispositioned divergence — a specific case where the encoded statute and the reference model produce different numbers, chased until someone can say why, and recorded together with the evidence for the explanation.
- "Explained" is a checked artifact, not a shrug: an arithmetic reconciliation, a citation to the governing text, or a documented convention of the reference model. A divergence nobody can account for is the one kind of result the process treats as unfinished.
- The explanation can land on either side. Sometimes the encoding is wrong (a misread threshold, a missing phase-out, a rate applied to the wrong base) and the fix goes into the encoding. Sometimes the reference model is wrong, out of date, or has made a defensible modeling choice the statute does not require, and the finding travels upstream — written up with its arithmetic and its citation and reported to UNU-WIDER, who maintain the SOUTHMOD models.
- The reference models are instruments, not adversaries; like any instrument they are improved by being read carefully against their source. Countries that never had a public model get one; the reference models get free, evidence-backed QA in return. Openness runs in both directions.
- A quiet reversal of trust: the usual assumption is that an established, decade-old reference model is the authority and a freshly generated encoding is on trial. Most of the time that is right and most findings are the encoding's to fix, but not all; when a divergence is chased to ground and the statute plainly says one thing while the reference model does another, the encoding becomes the instrument that caught it. Neither model is presumed correct; the statute is, and both are measured against it — nobody grades anybody, and everybody grades the law.
- Each country's ledger is a public GitHub discussion: Ghana's thirteen [@rulespecgh2026findings], Uganda's zero [@rulespecug2026findings], Zambia's eight [@rulespeczm2026findings], Ethiopia's two [@rulespecet2026findings], and Rwanda's five [NEEDS CITATION: Rwanda findings ledger URL].
- Zero findings is not the absence of a result; it is one. UGAMOD turned out statutorily exact everywhere the suite reached, and the encoding matched it to the shilling — established by evidence, not assumed by silence. A model checked against an independent reference and agreeing is in a different epistemic state from a model no one has checked, even when the two would print identical numbers. Uganda's zero is a compliment paid to a reference model, in evidence, and the most reassuring of the five results: the method can tell the difference between right and merely unexamined.

### The recipe and its cost
- The recipe was the same in every country: capture the governing acts from the official gazette, each provision anchored to a page in a signed source manifest so every rule traces back to the exact text it came from; encode the system one instrument at a time; put every change through the same merge-blocking gates the previous chapters described (if it does not compile, does not pass its proofs, does not clear the oracle comparison, it does not merge); run the oracle suites first case by case, then across a whole simulated population, so agreement holds not only on the examples someone thought to write but on the shape of the distribution; publish the findings ledger; report what it turned up upstream.
- Once a country reaches parity the gates ratchet: coverage can only rise and unexplained gaps can only fall, so the model cannot quietly rot back to wrong.
- Cost per country: days of agent time — not the years of institutional effort a national tax-benefit model has meant for the entire prior history of the field. Across five countries with five different statutes, the recipe barely changed.

### The moat was never only American
- Chapter 2 called it the analytical moat: government tax models run on the universe of actual returns while outside analysts get public-use files that are sampled, top-coded, and blurred, and no amount of open-source software fully closes the gap; that was told as an American story with an American statute, Section 6103, as its cause.
- But the moat was never only American, and in most of the world it is not really a moat at all — it is a vacuum: not a gap between the government's model and a challenger's, but the absence of any public, inspectable model on either side. A citizen of most countries cannot check what a tax change would do to a household like theirs, because no one outside a ministry has built the tool and often no one inside one has published it. The asymmetry Washington had (half a dozen institutions arguing over a tax bill within days of introduction) is, across most of the map, closer to silence.
- What a public model changes around a single tax decision: when a finance ministry proposes to broaden a VAT or replace a fuel subsidy with a cash grant, the question everyone cares about (who gains, who loses, by how much across the income distribution) lives inside a model; where the model is the ministry's alone the answer arrives as an assertion the opposition, press, civil society, and affected household can only accept or reject whole; where the model is public and checkable, the same people can run the reform themselves, disagree about assumptions in the open, and be caught when wrong. The point is not to take the tool from the ministry but to end its monopoly on being able to answer at all.
- What the July week changed is the marginal cost of ending that silence: building a national tax-benefit model went from a multi-year undertaking to a job of days, and there are fourteen SOUTHMOD-family countries with reference models standing ready to check the work. But a model that costs days and that no one can check is not an advance over a model that costs years — only a faster way to be confidently wrong. What traveled from Ghana to Uganda to Zambia was not the encoding speed alone but the discipline bolted to it (the gazette manifests, the merge gates, the oracle suites, the public ledger of everything that did not match). The verification shipped in the same box as the model — the entire point of the exercise.

### When the data can't travel
- Rules are the easy half: a statute is public by definition; encoding it needs the text and a way to check the result, and both have become cheap. The harder half is the people — a tax-benefit model is only as good as its picture of who lives under the rules (at what incomes, in what households, drawing which benefits), and that picture comes from microdata, where openness usually runs out.
- The asymmetry is structural and runs opposite to intuition: a statute is written to be public (promulgated, gazetted, binding precisely because anyone can read it), so encoding it in the open takes nothing meant to be hidden; microdata is the reverse — a record of actual people, protected by law and by promise, its value coming from exactly the granular individual detail that makes it impossible to release. Law is portable because it is already public; microdata is stuck because it is already private.
- The summer's second experiment asked a harder question: take the calibrated synthetic population the data layer builds for the United States — populace, the commons introduced in Part II — and adapt it to a country it was never built for, then grade it honestly against that country's real data. The United Kingdom was the natural test case because it has both an open reference model and a high-quality household survey to grade against. The adapted population was run through a pre-registered evaluation harness (scoring rules fixed and published before any answers were known) and scored against held-out ground truth from Britain's Family Resources Survey [NEEDS CITATION: UK transfer evaluation, pre-registered harness, July 2026].
- The verdict was mixed and was reported as mixed (see Quotes). Two specifics carried it:
  - A defect found and fixed: a stale data feed had zeroed out state pensions, and the error cascaded downstream into pension-credit calculations; it was caught, traced to its source, and corrected.
  - A defect found and deliberately left open: capital gains had been carried across one-to-one into a survey surface that captures almost none of them; the honest fix is not to copy realizations from one country into another but to model asset positions and the rules under which gains are realized — a rebuild, designed but not yet done.
- The transferred population is good at what surveys are good at and blind where surveys are blind, and pretending otherwise would have been easy and wrong.
- One smaller episode (part of why the verdict can be trusted): a summary statistic in an early draft of the write-up turned out to be fabricated — fluent, plausible, and unsupported by the run it claimed to describe — and it was caught at a verification gate before publication and struck. The gates are aimed first at the builders' own output.
- Where both halves can be built they close tightly: in Belgium the same machinery was calibrated to twenty-one administrative targets and hit all twenty-one within 1.8%; and on the same population, per-record social-security contributions agreed with the EUROMOD reference model to within three eurocents per person per year [NEEDS CITATION: Belgium calibration and EUROMOD per-record agreement]. Not a promise the model is right, but a small, checkable number stating exactly how close it came.

### An open referee (custodian packs)
- Belgium points at the harder case: for many countries the microdata cannot leave the building — national statistical offices hold survey and administrative records under legal obligations that do not bend for a research project; you cannot download the file and should not want to be able to. That looks like the end of open modeling in those places: you can encode their laws in the open but never open their data.
- The answer is to stop trying to move the data and to move the referee instead: give the institution that holds the microdata two things — the transferred population, and a pre-registered evaluation pack (the scoring code pinned to an exact version, the configuration frozen, and a scorecard schema that fixes in advance which numbers will be reported). The institution runs the pack inside its own walls, against its own protected records, and publishes only the scorecard: a short, low-dimensional set of results saying how well the transferred population matched the ground truth. Because the scorecard was specified before the run, no one can fish the data for a flattering statistic afterward; the referee's questions are fixed before it ever sees the answers. No record leaves the building; the score does.
- A scorecard is deliberately small — a handful of numbers, fixed in advance, saying how closely the transferred population reproduced the real one on the dimensions that matter (how the income distribution lines up, how many households the model places on each benefit, how far the tails diverge). It carries none of the underlying records and none of their risk, and it is exactly the low-dimensional summary a verification chain is allowed to end in. You do not need to see a country's microdata to trust a model of it; you need a referee who has seen it, a test written down before the answers were known, and a score published where anyone can read it. That is less than full openness, and it is enough.
- The open-referee thesis (see Quotes) dissolves the tension: the encoded law can be fully open because law is public; the microdata can stay fully closed because it must; what sits between them — the referee — is a small, honest, pre-committed test either party can run and everyone can read. The verification chain still terminates in something real: not a dataset anyone downloaded, but a scorecard a custodian ran and stands behind.
- Synthesis (why every step worked): the rules, the data, and the prediction never had to travel together — Ghana's statutes encoded in the open while Ghana's household records stayed home; a US-built population shipped to the UK and graded there; a statistical office running a referee no outsider was allowed to watch. Each move works only because law, population, and forecast are genuinely separable — built by different methods, checked against different ground truth, and best kept in different hands. So far the separation has been a technical convenience; the next chapter is about an organization that took it seriously enough to build itself around it.

## Arguments

1. Two conditions must both hold for the week to matter — a model can be built in days, AND each arrives carrying the evidence for whether it is right; the second is load-bearing, because a fast model no one can check is only a faster way to be confidently wrong.
2. A "finding" is a dispositioned divergence, not a bug report or a failure notice; the only disallowed result is a divergence nobody can account for.
3. Findings land on either side of the comparison; neither model is presumed correct — the statute is, and both are measured against it (nobody grades anybody; everybody grades the law).
4. Zero findings is a positive result, not an absence: a checked-and-agreeing model is in a different epistemic state from an unchecked one even when they would print identical numbers; the method can tell "right" from "merely unexamined."
5. The recipe is portable and cheap (days of agent time per country) and barely changed across five different statutes; the ratchet keeps a country at parity from rotting back to wrong.
6. The analytical moat (Chapter 2, Section 6103) was never only American; across most of the world it is a vacuum, not a moat, and a public model ends a ministry's monopoly on being able to answer who-gains-who-loses.
7. Encoding speed alone is not the advance; the advance is verification shipping in the same box as the model.
8. Rules and microdata are asymmetric by design: law is portable because already public; microdata is stuck because already private — so open modeling everywhere must be designed around that fact, not in spite of it.
9. A transferred population is good where surveys are good and blind where surveys are blind; honesty about the blindness (capital-gains defect left open, state-pension defect fixed, fabricated stat struck at the gate) is what makes the verdict trustworthy.
10. The open-referee construction resolves the rules-open / data-closed tension: a pre-registered evaluation pack run by the data custodian, publishing only a low-dimensional scorecard, is a valid terminus for the verification chain — you need a referee who has seen the data, a test fixed before the answers, and a published score, not the data itself.
11. The boundary question Nigeria poses: without an oracle or administrative ground truth the encoding can be built but the correctness claim stays weaker, and saying so in the same breath is part of the method — the boundary of the method is part of the method.

# Style read — front matter + Part I (2026-07-10)

*First-hand prose read of the preface, introduction, and chapters 1–4, prompted by
Max's question about the writing style of the chapters he hand-worked more. This is
about texture, not content — content verdicts are in `synthesis.md`.*

## The register

The default voice is high-quality explainer: clean, evenly lit, disciplined,
honest. Closer to a Stripe-Press/Works-in-Progress longform than to
narrative-nonfiction (no scene-craft, almost no sensory detail, people introduced
by credential rather than shown in motion). The restraint is the book's most
distinctive virtue and reads as genuine, not performed — random-walk-beats-CBO,
Meyer's survey-data crisis, and "Project Lorenz never fully materialized" are all
presented against interest, which almost no book in this genre does.

## Two textures, and the seam is visible

The hand-worked passages read differently from the assembled-explainer passages,
and better:

- **Explainer texture** (most of ch 1, 2, 4): hook → context → taxonomy → honest
  assessment → forward look. Uniform mid-length paragraphs, bold-led parallel
  lists, tidy triads, rhetorical-question transitions.
- **Hand texture** (ch 3's second half, parts of the preface): continuous prose,
  time-anchored specifics, no lists. "In June 2004—a month after my high school
  graduation, two months before I left our Menlo Park home for UC Berkeley—Alex
  suffered a spinal cord injury." That double time-stamp is real memoir instinct —
  the injury placed inside the family's calendar. IEOR 131, Finelite in Union City,
  pre-cut wire, the Basic Income subreddit, "So we did." The chapter slows down and
  pays attention, and it's the best sustained writing in Part I.

The revision implication: the personal texture is the book's scarcest resource.
Spend it more widely — not by adding memoir, but by giving other passages the same
concreteness and willingness to stay in a moment.

## The five recognizable tics (ranked by cost)

1. **Bold-led parallel lists, 2–4 per chapter.** "**Trust deficits.** …
   **Limited debate.** … **Expertise hoarding.** … **Democratic deficit.**" (ch 2);
   the promise/gap lists (ch 3); the failure modes (ch 4). Individually clear;
   cumulatively the PowerPoint shows through the prose. Ration to ~one per chapter
   and convert the rest to sequenced prose.
2. **One-line paragraph payoffs.** "But that paper planted a seed." / "This was a
   small revolution." / "The problem wasn't the math." Effective at 1–2 per
   chapter; current density is 5–8, so the mic-drops stop dropping. "So we did."
   is the best in the book because the chapter earns it — keep that one, cut half
   the rest.
3. **Heavy signposting.** "To understand what Orcutt was reacting against, you need
   to understand…" / "That story is the subject of Part II. But it only makes sense
   in the context of…" The book announces, does, then recaps. Cutting a third of
   the signposts would make it read more confident.
4. **Inventory middles.** Ch 1's "Family of Models" and ch 2's "Landscape Today"
   become annotated catalogs (model, institution, year, clause). Where a person
   gets one concrete artifact — Orcutt's home-built regression analyzer, Feenberg's
   four decades of quiet maintenance, Rivlin's advisor-student lineage — the page
   comes alive. Where they get a CV line (Steuerle and Burman "were tax policy
   veterans"), it doesn't. Rule of thumb: every named person gets one artifact,
   quote, or decision, or loses the name.
5. **Uniform sentence rhythm.** Variance comes almost entirely from the punch
   one-liners; there are few long cumulative sentences that build and almost no
   metaphor beyond the functional ("analytical moat" — the best coinage in Part I;
   "wall of frustration"). A handful of longer, running sentences in the right
   places would break the staccato-medium-staccato meter.

## What to protect in any rewrite

- The chapter openings — every one of the four lands ("In 1957, an economist named
  Guy Orcutt published a paper that almost nobody read").
- The honesty spine: against-interest findings, "approximately right, sometimes
  wrong, better than alternatives, always improvable," the George Box close
  ("epistemic hygiene").
- The Asimov inversion (ch 1), the §6103 moat (ch 2), the Alex passage and "So we
  did." (ch 3), the ACA component-vs-aggregate table and random-walk finding (ch 4).
- The no-purple-prose discipline. The fix for texture is specificity, not ornament.

## One-line characterization

Confident, honest explainer prose with an engineer's taxonomy habit — and, where
the author's hand pressed hardest, a quieter, more specific voice that is better
than the book's default and should become it.
